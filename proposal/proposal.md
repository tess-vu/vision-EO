# Final Project Proposal: Applying Topographically Aware U-Net Modeling on the 2025 Los Angeles Palisades Fire

**Tess Vu, Luciano Lu, Ming Cao**

## Problem Definition & Use Case

The Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI), as denoted by the US Fire Administration, is the "zone of transition between unoccupied land and human development where structures and other human development meet or intermingle with undeveloped wildland or vegetative fuels", and the January 2025 Los Angeles Palisades Fire that encompasses that paradigm was a particularly nationalized event that burned through steep, chaparral-dense canyons directly into densely populated residential grids. The problem this project seeks to solve is the rapid, highly accurate spatial delineation of destroyed urban infrastructure and severe vegetative canopy loss following a WUI fire event, relying strictly on passive optical satellite imagery.

The target users for this output are civic agencies, such as the Los Angeles County Department of Regional Planning, local utility providers, and environmental resilience initiatives. Currently, these stakeholders often rely on generalized drawn containment perimeters, which encompass large, unburned islands, because high-fidelity ecological data like the US Fire Service RAVG (Rapid Assessment of Vegetation Condition after Wildfire) is frequently unavailable for state and private lands. By generating a refined binary segmentation mask with 1 as High-Severity Burn / Destroyed Infrastructure and 0 as Unburned / Intact, this model will provide immediate, actionable intelligence to inform post-disaster zoning, soil stabilization deployments, and future WUI buffer planning.

## Technical Justification

To achieve precise spatial boundaries of disaster impact, this project frames the problem as a semantic segmentation task, significantly augmented by a hybrid data-engineering protocol to overcome noisy operational ground truth.

A positive prediction means the model has identified a contiguous area exhibiting both the spectral signature of severe burn, which is a drastic drop in Near-Infrared reflectance and a spike in Shortwave-Infrared reflectance, and the spatial morphology of disaster damage. Because the proposed U-Net will ingest ancillary data alongside optical bands, it is learning the contextual relationship between spectral burn signatures, local topography, and existing urban infrastructure footprints.

Semantic segmentation is the required task type because municipal planners need a pixel-perfect map of affected areas to overlay on property parcels. A simple image classification model (e.g. categorizing a whole tile as "burned" vs. "unburned") provides zero actionable spatial intelligence. A regression model predicting a continuous severity score is theoretically useful but overly complex for immediate civic triage, which requires a definitive binary boundary for resource allocation.

However, this comes with several obstacles, like relying on passive optical satellites and operational ground truth tends to introduce physical and bureaucratic constraints that the model architecture must explicitly solve:

The first constraint is topographic shadowing. The Palisades fire occurred in January and during the northern hemisphere winter, the sun sits low in the southern sky. When Sentinel-2 passes over the Santa Monica Mountains at mid-morning, steep north-facing canyons are cast in deep shadow. In passive optical sensing, these shadows return near-zero reflectance across all bands. This mathematically mimics the spectral signature of a severe burn, causing traditional spectral indices to hallucinate massive false-positive burn scars.

The second constraint is label noise from operational data, because federal RAVG data is unavailable for this jurisdiction, the project must rely on Los Angeles County / NIFC containment perimeters. These polygons map where firefighters established control lines, not strictly what burned, often enveloping entire unburned neighborhoods. If fed directly into a neural network as the target mask (i.e. _Y_), the model will learn that intact, healthy vegetation is "destroyed", fatally degrading the model's precision.

## Methodological Precedent

- Ngoua Ndong Avele, J. B., & Goryainov, V. S. (2025). Wildfire Damage Assessment over Eaton Canyon, California, Using Radar and Multispectral Datasets from Sentinel Satellites and Machine Learning Methods. _Environmental and Earth Sciences Proceedings_, 36(1), 6. <https://doi.org/10.3390/eesp2025036006>
  - They used a Random Forest algorithm to classify fire severity and analyze post-fire vegetation where they calculated several indices like dNBR, RBR, and RdNBR where they did a fused approach combining them. The data sources include multispectral imagery from Sentinel-2 L2A's NIR and SWIR bands for pre-fire and post-fire as well as SAR from Sentinel. They used the Kappa index and confusion matrices to compare individual indices with the combined approach. Limitations they noted was a debate on which burn metrics best match field data because previous literature was inconclusive and numeric thresholds are often determined empirically depending on ecosystem. For this Palisades project, this study validates using Sentinel-2 to calculate dNBR as a baseline, and their fused approach justifies the decision to try and move beyond a dNBR baseline and target a multi-modal approach to create a multi-channel U-Net tensor.
- Miguel M. Pinto, Renata Libonati, Ricardo M. Trigo, Isabel F. Trigo, Carlos C. DaCamara, A deep learning approach for mapping and dating burned areas using temporal sequences of satellite images, _ISPRS Journal of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing_, Volume 160, 2020, Pages 260-274, ISSN 0924-2716, <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.isprsjprs.2019.12.014>
  - This paper used a U-Net CNN with an encoder from VGG19 architecture to do image segmentation for Sentinel-1 surface water mapping. It compared two automated data labeling methods for model training with JRC maps versus an Otsu variant approach, and did hyperparameter tuning to evaluate the different learning rates and loss functions. The data sources include Sentinel-1 SAR products with training labels from JRC and Edge-Otsu, with independent validation from high-resolution Planet Scope imagery. The performance was measured using the f1-Score, Cohen Kappa, and confusion matrices using a stratified K-Fold CV method as well as statistical significance tests. Of the limitations, the authors mentioned high compute costs that restricted more model comparisons, limited temporal range and geographic scope for independent validation, and radiometric distortions in SAR due to high-relief mountain areas. This informs the Palisades project by providing the precedent for using a U-Net architecture for segmentation tasks. It also validates using Otsu as a method for automating binary labeled target masks when manual methods create an extensive workload.
- Timothy Mayer, Ate Poortinga, Biplov Bhandari, Andrea P. Nicolau, Kel Markert, Nyein Soe Thwal, Amanda Markert, Arjen Haag, John Kilbride, Farrukh Chishtie, Amit Wadhwa, Nicholas Clinton, David Saah, Deep learning approach for Sentinel-1 surface water mapping leveraging Google Earth Engine, _ISPRS Open Journal of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing_, Volume 2, 2021, 100005, ISSN 2667-3932, <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ophoto.2021.100005>
  - This paper proposed a DL model dubbed BA-Net based on U-Net architecture to filter noise and capture longer-term temporal dependencies in satellite data. Their sources include daily atmospheric observations from infrared imaging at 750m resolution alongside VIIRS 375m fire product. The MCD64A1 collection was used as a burned area target dataset for training, with a variety of other satellites like Landsat-8, MTBS, etc. acting as validation. The performance was evaluated by the Dice Coefficient and Overall Accuracy, with temporal validation using MAE compared to active fire. The authors noted limitations related to the coarse spatial resolution that made it difficult to detect smaller fires, they also noted lack of environmental variables like land cover and topography that could help explain differences in fires across regions. Their lack of topography informs this Palisades proposal to try and use DEM Aspect array and damaged building footprints to be fed into the U-Net tensor to try and resolve the topographic shadow issue regarding WUI.

There's not much literature on whether feeding post-burn damage building footprints nor feeding DEM Aspect/Hillshade improves U-Net accuracy, so this is the gap that remains to be seen and will be addressed by this project.

## Data Plan

The project will construct a multi-channel tensor fusing passive optical, topographical, and civic vector data, strictly bounded by the geography of the Santa Monica Mountains and Pacific Palisades (Jan-Feb 2025).

**Primary Optical Data (_X<sub>1</sub>_):** Sentinel-2 Level-2A imagery, accessed via Element 84 Earth Search STAC API with resolution between 10m-20m. The required features are the pre-fire and post-fire Near-Infrared (Band 8A) and Shortwave-Infrared (Band 12) bands.

**Topographic Ancillary Data (_X<sub>2</sub>_):** USGS 3DEP 10m Digital Elevation Model processed into Aspect and Hillshade arrays to counter the topographic shadow issue.

**Civic Ancillary Data (_X<sub>3</sub>_):** [Los Angeles County Open Data Building Footprints](https://geohub.lacity.org/datasets/lahub::building-footprints/explore?location=34.038181%2C-118.456339%2C12) rasterized to match the 10m Sentinel grid to provide the WUI context.

**Labels (_Y_):** The [Los Angeles County Dissolved Fire Perimeter](https://data.lacounty.gov/datasets/lacounty::palisades-and-eaton-dissolved-fire-perimeters-2025/explore?layer=1&location=34.104210%2C-118.391634%2C9) will serve as a definitive Area of Interest (AOI). To eliminate the "unburned island" label noise, a delta Normalized Burn Ratio (dNBR) threshold, combined with [post-fire structural footprints](https://gis.data.cnra.ca.gov/datasets/CALFIRE-Forestry::cal-fire-damage-inspection-dins-data/explore?location=34.051011%2C-118.539485%2C13), will be applied exclusively within this AOI to extract a clean binary target mask prior to U-Net training. This is calculated using a Boolean union, so a pixel is 1 if it passes the Otsu vegetation threshold or if it contains a point geometry from CALFIRE that's noted as "destroyed".

**Backup Plan:** Passive optical sensors are dependent on clear skies, so if the post-fire temporal window in late January 2025 exhibits heavy cloud cover over Los Angeles during Sentinel-2 overpasses, the backup plan is to query the Landsat 8 and 9 STAC catalogs (Collection 2 Surface Reflectance) to find a cloud-free acquisition, adjusting the spatial resolution to 30m.

## Modeling Approach

Before implementing deep learning, the project will establish a performance floor using standard spectral thresholding where the baseline model will calculate the dNBR using the Sentinel-2 NIR and SWIR bands across the entire study area, then an Otsu automatic threshold will be applied to generate a binary mask. This non-deep-learning approach is mathematically expected to fail by identifying steep, unburned winter shadows as fire damage, and failing to segment unburned infrastructure from surrounding ash, providing a clear delta for the neural network.

The primary model will be the topographically aware U-Net semantic segmentation model where preprocessing will include all optical bands, DEM derivatives, and vector footprints that will be clipped, reprojected to the exact same UTM coordinate reference system, and normalized. As for feature engineering and the input tensor, it will be a multi-channel array stacked as: \[dNBR, Post-Fire SWIR, DEM Aspect, Building Footprints\].

The U-Net's encoder will capture the spatial context of the burn scars relative to the infrastructure and canyon slopes. And by feeding the topography explicitly into the network, the model learns the spatial relationship between the sun angle and the terrain, resolving the winter shadow failure mode while also isolating destroyed WUI infrastructure.

## Evaluation Strategy

Given the spatial nature of the problem, standard global accuracy is an inappropriate metric due to massive class imbalance as most of Los Angeles County did not burn. A model predicting "unburned" for every pixel would achieve high statistical accuracy, but zero practical utility.

Regarding the metrics, the model will be strictly evaluated using the Intersection over Union (IoU) and the F1-Score (Dice Coefficient), focusing exclusively on the positive burned/destroyed minority class. The reasoning behind this is that these metrics heavily penalize false positives (hallucinating burns in shadows) and false negatives (missing destroyed homes).

For the data split, the dataset will be divided using a topographically stratified spatial cross-validation approach with a 70% train, 15% validation, and 15% test split. This is because a random pixel split could inadvertently put all flat coastal areas in the training set and all steep canyons in the validation set, so the spatial blocks will be explicitly engineered to ensure the network trains on both WUI flats and heavily shadowed ravines.

On the practical utility, for a civic agency like LA County Regional Planning, an F1-score exceeding 0.85 on the test set is required. At this threshold, the model serves as a reliable, rapid-response tool for estimating structural loss and directing environmental remediation teams to high-risk slopes before subsequent winter rains trigger catastrophic debris flows.
