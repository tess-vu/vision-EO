# Final Project Proposal: Applying Topographically Aware U-Net Modeling on the 2025 Los Angeles Palisades Fire

**Tess Vu, Luciano Lu, Ming Cao**

## Problem Definition & Use Case

The Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI), as denoted by the US Fire Administration, is the "zone of transition between unoccupied land and human development where structures and other human development meet or intermingle with undeveloped wildland or vegetative fuels", and the January 2025 Los Angeles Palisades Fire that encompasses that paradigm was a particularly nationalized event that burned through steep, chaparral-dense canyons directly into densely populated residential grids. The problem this project seeks to solve is the rapid, highly accurate spatial prediction of destroyed urban infrastructure following a WUI fire event, using passive optical satellite imagery and topographic context as predictive features.

The target users for this output are civic agencies, such as the Los Angeles County Department of Regional Planning, local utility providers, and environmental resilience initiatives. Currently, these stakeholders must wait for labor-intensive field inspections before they can allocate rebuilding resources, estimate insurance liabilities, or deploy soil stabilization teams. By generating a refined binary segmentation mask predicting structural damage from remotely sensed spectral signatures, this model will provide immediate, actionable intelligence to inform post-disaster zoning, debris removal prioritization, and future WUI buffer planning, before complete field assessments are available.

## Technical Justification

To achieve precise spatial predictions of structural damage, this project frames the problem as a semantic segmentation task where the model learns to associate spectral burn signatures, topographic context, and building footprint presence with field-verified structural damage outcomes.

A positive prediction means the model has identified a pixel where the combination of spectral signatures (elevated dNBR indicating vegetation loss and increased SWIR reflectance from ash/debris), topographic position (aspect relative to fire spread), and infrastructure presence correlates with CAL FIRE Damage Inspection (DINS) field assessments of structural destruction. The proposed U-Net learns whether the observable remote sensing features can predict the ground-truth damage outcomes recorded by trained field assessors.

Semantic segmentation is the required task type because municipal planners need a pixel-perfect map of predicted damage to overlay on property parcels. A simple image classification model (e.g. categorizing a whole tile as "damaged" vs. "undamaged") provides zero actionable spatial intelligence. A regression model predicting a continuous severity score is theoretically useful but overly complex for immediate civic triage, which requires a definitive binary boundary for resource allocation.

However, this prediction task comes with several obstacles that the model architecture must explicitly address:

The first constraint is topographic shadowing. The Palisades fire occurred in January and during the northern hemisphere winter, the sun sits low in the southern sky. When Sentinel-2 passes over the Santa Monica Mountains at mid-morning, steep north-facing canyons are cast in deep shadow. In passive optical sensing, these shadows return near-zero reflectance across all bands. This mathematically mimics the spectral signature of a severe burn, potentially causing the model to hallucinate damage predictions in shadowed but undamaged areas. By explicitly feeding DEM-derived aspect into the model, the network can learn to distinguish shadow artifacts from genuine burn signatures.

The second constraint is the sparse and clustered nature of structural damage. Destroyed buildings represent a small fraction of the total fire perimeter area, creating severe class imbalance. Buildings cluster along specific road networks and topographic positions within the WUI, meaning the spatial distribution of damage is highly non-random. The model must learn that structural damage requires both the presence of a building footprint and spectral evidence of fire impact, neither alone is sufficient.

The third constraint is maintaining methodological validity and robustness. Using spectral indices like dNBR as both input features and label-generation mechanisms would create target leakage, where the model subtly learns to replicate a threshold rather than predict genuine outcomes. This project avoids this pitfall by using independently collected CAL FIRE DINS field inspection data as ground truth, making sure the model learns predictive relationships rather than circular definitions.

## Methodological Precedent

- Ngoua Ndong Avele, J. B., & Goryainov, V. S. (2025). Wildfire Damage Assessment over Eaton Canyon, California, Using Radar and Multispectral Datasets from Sentinel Satellites and Machine Learning Methods. _Environmental and Earth Sciences Proceedings_, 36(1), 6. <https://doi.org/10.3390/eesp2025036006>
  - They used a Random Forest algorithm to classify fire severity and analyze post-fire vegetation where they calculated several indices like dNBR, RBR, and RdNBR where they did a fused approach combining them. The data sources include multispectral imagery from Sentinel-2 L2A's NIR and SWIR bands for pre-fire and post-fire as well as SAR from Sentinel. They used the Kappa index and confusion matrices to compare individual indices with the combined approach. Limitations they noted was a debate on which burn metrics best match field data because previous literature was inconclusive and numeric thresholds are often determined empirically depending on ecosystem. For this Palisades project, this study validates using Sentinel-2 to calculate dNBR as a predictive feature, and their fused approach justifies the decision to create a multi-channel U-Net tensor combining spectral indices with ancillary data.

- Miguel M. Pinto, Renata Libonati, Ricardo M. Trigo, Isabel F. Trigo, Carlos C. DaCamara, A deep learning approach for mapping and dating burned areas using temporal sequences of satellite images, _ISPRS Journal of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing_, Volume 160, 2020, Pages 260-274, ISSN 0924-2716, <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.isprsjprs.2019.12.014>
  - This paper used a U-Net CNN with an encoder from VGG19 architecture to do image segmentation for burned area mapping. It compared two automated data labeling methods for model training with official fire products versus algorithm-derived labels, and did hyperparameter tuning to evaluate different learning rates and loss functions. The performance was measured using the F1-Score, Cohen Kappa, and confusion matrices using a stratified K-Fold CV method as well as statistical significance tests. Of the limitations, the authors mentioned high compute costs that restricted more model comparisons, limited temporal range and geographic scope for independent validation, and radiometric distortions due to high-relief mountain areas. This informs the Palisades project by providing the precedent for using a U-Net architecture for fire-related segmentation tasks, and highlights the importance of using independently validated ground truth rather than algorithmically-derived labels that could introduce circular dependencies.

- Timothy Mayer, Ate Poortinga, Biplov Bhandari, Andrea P. Nicolau, Kel Markert, Nyein Soe Thwal, Amanda Markert, Arjen Haag, John Kilbride, Farrukh Chishtie, Amit Wadhwa, Nicholas Clinton, David Saah, Deep learning approach for Sentinel-1 surface water mapping leveraging Google Earth Engine, _ISPRS Open Journal of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing_, Volume 2, 2021, 100005, ISSN 2667-3932, <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ophoto.2021.100005>
  - This paper proposed a DL model dubbed BA-Net based on U-Net architecture to filter noise and capture spatial dependencies in satellite data. Their sources include daily atmospheric observations from infrared imaging at 750m resolution alongside VIIRS 375m fire product. The MCD64A1 collection was used as a burned area target dataset for training, with a variety of other satellites like Landsat-8, MTBS, etc. acting as validation. The performance was evaluated by the Dice Coefficient and Overall Accuracy. The authors noted limitations related to the coarse spatial resolution that made it difficult to detect smaller fires, and the lack of environmental variables like land cover and topography that could help explain differences in fire behavior across regions. Their identified gap regarding topography directly informs this Palisades project to incorporate DEM Aspect into the U-Net tensor to address the topographic shadow issue in WUI environments.

There is limited literature on using field-collected structural damage assessments, like CAL FIRE DINS, as ground truth for remote sensing models, or on whether feeding DEM Aspect explicitly improves prediction of WUI structural damage. This gap will be addressed by this project in addition to a practical use-case for planners to anticipate areas that may be more susceptible to wildfire disasters or specifically sending aid to buildings with predicted damage.

## Data Plan

The project will construct a multi-channel tensor fusing passive optical, topographical, and civic vector data, strictly bounded by the geography of the Santa Monica Mountains and Pacific Palisades (Jan-Feb 2025).

**Primary Optical Data (_X<sub>1</sub>_):** Sentinel-2 Level-2A imagery, accessed via Element 84 Earth Search STAC API with resolution between 10m-20m. The required features are the pre-fire and post-fire Near-Infrared (Band 8A) and Shortwave-Infrared (Band 12) bands, used to compute the delta Normalized Burn Ratio (dNBR) as a predictive feature.

**Topographic Ancillary Data (_X<sub>2</sub>_):** USGS 3DEP 10m Digital Elevation Model processed into an Aspect array to address the topographic shadow constraint by providing the model with explicit information about slope orientation relative to sun angle.

**Civic Ancillary Data (_X<sub>3</sub>_):** [Microsoft US Building Footprints](https://github.com/microsoft/USBuildingFootprints) rasterized to match the 20m Sentinel grid to provide explicit WUI infrastructure context, indicating where structures exist that could potentially be damaged.

**Labels (_Y_):** The [CAL FIRE Damage Inspection (DINS) Data](https://gis.data.cnra.ca.gov/datasets/CALFIRE-Forestry::cal-fire-damage-inspection-dins-data/explore?location=34.051011%2C-118.539485%2C13) will serve as the ground truth for structural damage. DINS points represent structures physically inspected and classified by trained CAL FIRE assessors after the fire event. Points classified as "Destroyed (>50%)", "Major (25-50%)", "Minor (10-25%)", "Affected (>0-10%)", or "Inaccessible" will be spatially joined to Microsoft Building Footprints with a 15-meter buffer to account for GPS drift, then rasterized to create a binary target mask where 1 indicates a damaged structure footprint and 0 indicates intact/no structure.

The [Los Angeles County Dissolved Fire Perimeter](https://data.lacounty.gov/datasets/lacounty::palisades-and-eaton-dissolved-fire-perimeters-2025/explore?layer=1&location=34.104210%2C-118.391634%2C9) will serve as the Area of Interest (AOI) boundary, restricting analysis to the fire-affected region.

**Methodological Note:** This ground truth approach deliberately avoids using spectral indices (dNBR, Otsu thresholding) as part of the label generation process. Since dNBR is an input feature, using it to construct labels would create target leakage where the model learns to replicate a threshold rather than predict genuine damage outcomes. The DINS field assessments provide an independently collected ground truth that enables valid evaluation of whether remote sensing features can predict structural damage.

**Backup Plan:** Passive optical sensors are dependent on clear skies, so if the post-fire temporal window in late January 2025 exhibits heavy cloud cover over Los Angeles during Sentinel-2 overpasses, the backup plan is to query the Landsat 8 and 9 STAC catalogs (Collection 2 Surface Reflectance) to find a cloud-free acquisition, adjusting the spatial resolution to 30m.

## Modeling Approach

Before implementing deep learning, the project will establish a performance floor using a Random Forest pixel classifier. This baseline model treats each pixel independently, using the four-channel feature tensor (dNBR, Post-Fire SWIR, DEM Aspect, Building Footprints) to predict structural damage without considering spatial context. Because Random Forest cannot leverage the spatial relationships between neighboring pixels, it establishes a lower bound that the U-Net should exceed if spatial context is valuable for this prediction task.

The primary model will be the topographically aware U-Net semantic segmentation model. Preprocessing will include all optical bands, DEM derivatives, and vector footprints that will be clipped, reprojected to the exact same UTM coordinate reference system (EPSG:32611), and normalized. The input tensor will be a multi-channel array stacked as: \[dNBR, Post-Fire SWIR, DEM Aspect, Building Footprints\].

The U-Net's encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections enables the model to capture both fine-grained pixel-level features and broader spatial context. By feeding the topography explicitly into the network alongside spectral features and building footprints, the model can learn complex relationships — for example, that high dNBR values on north-facing slopes with buildings present predict structural damage, while high dNBR values on north-facing slopes without buildings may indicate shadow artifacts rather than fire impact.

Given the severe class imbalance inherent in DINS-only ground truth (damaged structures represent approximately 2-5% of pixels within the fire perimeter), the model will employ a combined Dice Loss and Focal Loss function. Dice Loss directly optimizes the F1-Score (Dice Coefficient), while Focal Loss down-weights easy-to-classify background pixels to focus learning on the minority damage class. Training patches containing damaged structures will be oversampled to ensure the network sees sufficient positive examples during training.

## Evaluation Strategy

Given the spatial nature of the problem and severe class imbalance, standard global accuracy is an inappropriate metric. A model predicting "undamaged" for every pixel would achieve high statistical accuracy (>95%) but zero practical utility.

Regarding the metrics, the model will be strictly evaluated using the Intersection over Union (IoU) and the F1-Score (Dice Coefficient), focusing exclusively on the positive damaged minority class. These metrics heavily penalize false positives (predicting damage where none exists) and false negatives (missing actual destroyed structures), providing a balanced assessment of model utility.

For the data split, the dataset will be divided using a topographically stratified spatial cross-validation approach with a 70% train, 15% validation, and 15% test split. Spatial blocks (64×64 pixels) will be assigned to splits to prevent spatial autocorrelation leakage, where adjacent pixels in different splits would allow the model to trivially interpolate. Blocks containing damaged structures will be stratified across splits to ensure each split contains a representative proportion of the minority class.

On the practical utility, this task is genuinely challenging because the model must learn whether spectral and topographic features correlate with independently-assessed structural damage, a predictive relationship that may or may not exist at the resolution and signal-to-noise ratio of Sentinel-2 imagery. Success would be defined as the U-Net achieving a meaningful improvement over the Random Forest baseline, demonstrating that spatial context provides value for structural damage prediction. An F1-score exceeding 0.50 on the held-out test set would indicate the remote sensing features carry predictive signal for structural damage, while scores approaching 0.70+ would suggest the model could serve as a useful rapid-assessment tool for civic agencies awaiting complete field inspections.
